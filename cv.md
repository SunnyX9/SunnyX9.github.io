Curriculum Vitae | July 2026

__________________________________________________________________________________

I am a computational social scientist working at the intersection of sociology and computer science, with specific expertise in applied machine learning and AI in social research. My current work focuses on how ML and AI methods can be leveraged in the study of the human life course, to further understand the complex inequalities and disadvantages for labor market outcomes. I am finishing my PhD in this topic in 2026.

in progress	Kuikka, S. Unpacking the (Un)Predictability of Early (Un)Employment (tentative title). Follow-up on Kuikka (2024), studying the combinations of variables that together predict (un)employment, as well as which combinations drive the ML model to make wrong predictions, in the context of Finland. Principal Component Analysis is the main tool, literature combining several social scientific theorical approaches. 
in progress	Kuikka S. Predicting Lifetime Disposable Income with Joint Family Background Characteristics (tentative title). Exploring tree-based ML algorithms in predicting lifetime disposable income, and arguing for the fit of the models in capturing theoretical plurality, over the standard ones used in the social mobility literature. The results uncover macro predictability of the outcome, as well as describe sub-groups that separately predict each outcome class. The study leverages Swedish longitudinal population registers. 
in progress	Kuikka S. & Thaning, M. Bounded Measure for Inequality of Opportunity (IOp) in Income (tentative title). The study leverages tools both traditional (fixed effects) and contemporary (machine learning) for bounded measurement for IOp in income, using detailed measurements from longitudinal individual-level population registers, and deriving a lifetime disposable income measurement averaged across approx. 3 decades. 
in progress	Kuikka S. & Kim, W. Differing Predictors for Education-Occupation Mismatch Between the Migrant and Native Populations in Sweden (tentative title). Using ML tools for understanding the complex set of predictors for education-occupation mismatch, that likely differs between the migrant and native populations, in the context of Sweden. The study leverages individual-level and longitudinal population register data, and scopes several theories related to transition into the labor market, and education-occupation mismatch. 
in progress	Sivak, L., Stulp, G. […] Kuikka, S. Births are difficult to predict even with rich survey and full-population register data (tentative title). A community paper reporting the results from Predicting Fertility data challenge, a mass collaboration aiming to measure the predictability of fertility outcomes in the context of The Netherlands. The study had several interdisciplinary research teams working on the same data (panel study & population registers) and the same task (predicting who will have a(nother) child in the near future).
